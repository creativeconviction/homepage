An interview with Paul Jun

Brand engineers and the future of creative teams

Creative Director, Brand and Tooling at Ramp

July 2026

Paul Jun leads brand design at Ramp, where he owns the brand identity, the visual and verbal language, and the tooling and systems that touch the brand across a company of nearly 1,800 people. He has spent seventeen years in the industry, and for most of that time he was frustrated by how tech treated brand design. His view is that AI is the change agent that finally shifts that perception, and he wants to be one of the people who shows what is possible.

Paul has completely crossed the line from designer to designer and builder. A month after Cursor came out, he built a Figma plugin in days, and he’s now rebuilt Ramp’s tone of voice in between meetings with Claude Code and the Figma MCP. He is now building Slack bots, review agents, and skill libraries so that anyone at Ramp can ship near-finished work without a designer in the room.

The conversation moves through why brand and product were ever split apart, what happens when a brand team stops solving requests and starts seeking opportunities, and why Paul thinks designers are uniquely equipped for a world where one person can build a product end to end. He calls this the dial-up era of AI, and he is clearly enjoying being at the front of the roller coaster.

LF + RA

What changes when brand reports into product and engineering versus when it reports into marketing? And what does that look like at Ramp today?

Paul

Typically a lot of startups place brand under marketing, and it’s a strange thing. At the end of the day, product design and brand design is just design. Product design is form follows function, brand design is form follows fantasy. Both require form, and both require a customer to experience that.

In my past two jobs before Ramp, I always negotiated that I would report to a designer and a builder, not a CMO. My team still works with marketing — we create the templates, the guidelines, the assets. But before the age of AI, most leaders and executives didn’t understand branding, so they just placed brand designers under marketing. This idea of an in-house agency was a trend for the last 20 years, and it’s not working — you’re going to lose a lot of good designers that way.

LF + RA

You mentioned startups move through phases. Can you walk through those?

Paul

Every startup goes through phases.

The first phase is PMF, where you just have to find product market fit. Brand design is influencing marketing campaigns, emails, images, the identity, the web. These things matter.

The second phase is a features war. One company builds a feature, another company says they have it too, but better. Companies get stuck in this world trying to out-feature each other, and it can be a race to the bottom.

The third phase is unique. You’re no longer just shipping features — you’re investing in experience. That’s really asking what the product feels like when you use it — not just what job it’s doing, but how it feels when you do the job. I think brand designers are the most poised to figure out what that means. My team at Ramp is accountable for the entire experience, from the first performance ad to the homepage, to a demo, to a sales conversation, the sales deck, the language the sales team uses, all the way up to onboarding into the product, your first moment of value, and inviting a colleague. That’s all brand. I’m not saying it all falls on the brand team, but rather everyone at a company should feel accountable for the brand.

LF + RA

You said most product designers don’t think this way, that they’re too narrow, and that only a handful can do both. Why is that, and how do people upskill into being an all-encompassing brand and product designer?

Paul

The best designers can do both product design and brand design. These people can build you an entire product, but they can also think about the motion states, how color is used, how typography is used, what the onboarding feels like, what the upsell feels like. Most product designers think very narrowly. Brand designers elevate it by thinking about the feeling of the experience, and not just in that one product moment, but the entire experience.

This is not the best answer, but some of it is luck. You have to work at a place with the right design leadership, somewhere that shows you can use both sides of your brain. I have a maybe cynical take, but I think the growth era, when everything was about moving fast in a low interest rate economy, pushed product design into a shallow game. That’s why product design became so boring and why everything looks the same. We figured out how to make frictionless apps, and product designers were held to numbers and outcomes they had to game at big companies. It created bad habits. They were forced to play the wrong game, and they never learned brand because they were stuck in their own world, managed by PMs or engineering teams, teams that never talked to each other.

LF + RA

Where do you think the divide between brand and product actually came from?

Paul

Design was born in Photoshop, and so was brand design. But at some point a tech company said “product design, you go over there and work with eng,” and “brand design, you more artistic types, go work with marketing.”

A lot of this came from FAANG companies. Many startups are social animals — they look at what’s working and copy it. They saw the big companies separate brand and product, so they did the same, and it created this weird expectation. I hope it completely changes. There’s a parallel with designers learning to code. There was a period early in my career where everyone said designers had to learn to code, and the ones who did were able to differentiate themselves. With AI, it’s no longer an excuse.

LF + RA

We’ve been arguing that brand comes from the founding team’s vision and is the product made visible, that it’s an extension of both product and team. Do you agree, or push back?

Paul

I think so. Even when I talk to prospects or agencies, every single one mentions Ramp’s brand as iconic. The team that built it before me did a great job creating something truly differentiated in the financial space, which is often esoteric, intimidating, high barrier to entry, with obscure language. A lot of those identities feel too intense. Ramp is quiet and confident, not loud and in your face. I take no credit for that.

The other thing that brought me to Ramp was that the engineering is clearly world class. When I was interviewing, every friend I told said Ramp was next level, that they have some of the best engineers. It’s clear because the product just works. It’s insanely great, and it’s that fast and automatic.

Now Ramp is scaling up, reaching new audiences, building new verticals. It’s like performing a symphony. When you’re a startup, you can sound like a fifth grade band recital and still succeed. Now it’s about how we continuously ship things that are on brand and meet the customer’s needs. I always say brand is behavior. What are the things we do that get talked about within the cultures we want to be part of? How do our ideas make it into that culture so other people start referencing our ways of working and thinking?

LF + RA

We’re seeing how sub-processes of brand are becoming programmable, while some parts of cohesive narrative building still need creatives in the loop. Does that resonate with what you’re seeing at Ramp?

Paul

We need to make the brand easy to love and impossible to misuse. We’re building a Slack bot right now that can generate a brief for a product area, turn that brief into a deck, then turn that deck into different assets, all inside Slack. I’m working with just one design engineer on it.

I have a Notion doc where I break down the requests my team gets and tier them. Tier one is high craft, the biggest launches and bets in the company. A tier three might be a LinkedIn image promoting that we have stablecoins. Someone should be able to do that in five minutes. Before AI, that might have taken a brand designer a full day with multiple back and forths. I want a world where no one ever has to touch a tier three asset again, maybe even as bold as no one touching a tier two asset, so that all the human talent and energy is dedicated to the biggest bets and the future.

LF + RA

How does brand work start to shift once more AI is utilized more?

Paul

There are two modes brand teams work in. Problem solving is asking what’s wrong. Opportunity seeking is asking what if. I’d say 99 percent of their energy is spent problem solving, doing other people’s requests — “make me a deck, make me a sticker, I need swag, resize this logo on my sales deck.” A lot of designers waste time on that. My goal is to flip it completely, so we’re spending time thinking about the future and building it, almost trying to wreck ourselves, because if we figure that out before our competitors, we stay ahead.

LF + RA

Is this a good moment for creatives?

Paul

For the first time in my career I feel like I’m ahead of the curve, which I’ve never felt in my life. The things I can do now in Claude Code blow my mind. I built the entire Ramp tone of voice in between meetings with Claude Code and the Figma MCP. That would have taken an agency six to eight weeks and $100k+.

But it’s not just the tools. You also have to have the ambition to execute on it. There are so many people in the bucket of fear who don’t want to touch it, who say it isn’t quality. I’ve onboarded so many friends who were skeptics, who read the wrong threads and let the wrong ideas into their brains. But when I sit them down and show them something on their screen they couldn’t have built before, it blows their mind. If that doesn’t excite you, you’re in the wrong field. We’re in the dial-up phase of AI right now, the AOL.com phase. It’s very early, and the more time goes on, the more overwhelmed people get. A lot of roles are going to be compressed, but I’m excited for designers, because they’re well equipped to live in a world where they can do everything end to end. A good brand product designer with deep experience can build a product, brand it, write for it, build the website, build the marketing materials, and make the product better over time. Designers are uniquely positioned to become that.

Rapid fire

I
Which companies feel aligned with how you think about moving fast and creativity?

Shopify. They’ve always been design minded, but they’ve turned something around in the last two years. Vercel was quite early too, with a great design team. Honestly though, I feel like most of the market is behind.

II
When did it click for you that everything was about to change?

End of 2023. I had a week or two off and downloaded Cursor, told myself I’d push through no matter how frustrated I got. In three days I built a Figma plugin I couldn’t believe I’d built. I sent it to an engineer and he called me asking how I knew how to code.

III
What’s the one belief you keep repeating to your team?

Make the brand easy to love and impossible to misuse.
